How to draw a graph?

On the right, the dodecahedron The dodecahedron graph is the graph formed by the vertices and edges of the dodecahedron, one of the five platonic solids.
 graph is loaded. One face of the dodecahedron is drawn as a regular pentagon (the 5 red nodes); the remaining nodes are put in random position. The five red nodes are fixed, the others can move.

Click on the Run button. The edges contract until the system reaches equilibrium Working out the physics: the energy of the system is defined by:
∑ i,j∈E(G) (xi−xj)2 + ( yi − yj )2 , where ( xi , yi ) is the position of node i in the plane. Taking gradients it is easy to derive the equilibrium condition: every node that is not nailed down must be at the center of gravity of its neighbors. In formula: 1 deg(i) ∑ j∈N(i) xj = xi and 1 deg(i) ∑ j∈N(i) yj = yi . The edges don't cross any more, and we get a pretty drawing of the dodecahedron.

Tutte proved the following nontrivial fact:

Nail the nodes of any face of a 3-connected planar graph to a convex polygon, and replace the edges by rubber bands. The equilibrium provides an embedding in the plane.

This embedding has very nice properties: all the edges are straight line segments, and all the faces are convex polygons.

Is this a good method to display planar 3-connected graphs?

Often it is, but there are some bad examples. Load the graph named "triangles" and click Run: This simple graph is drawn in the plane, but most of its nodes are crowded near the middle of the bottom. The "pipe" graph gives a similar example.

What to do if a planar graph is not 3-connected?

We can add edges to it to make it 3-connected, and delete these at the end. As a special case, a tree can be drawn nicely by fixing its leaves. Try the "tree5" graph.

Is this a good method to display nonplanar graphs? Sometimes; load and run the Petersen graph to see a nice drawing of this very famous and very symmetric nonplanar graph.
