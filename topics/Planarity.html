How to draw a graph?

On the left, the dodecahedron graph is loaded. One face of the dodecahedron is drawn as a regular pentagon (the 5 red nodes); the remaining nodes are put in random position. The five red nodes are fixed, the others can move.

Click on the button Move. The edges contract until the system reaches equilibrium. The edges don’t cross any more, and we get a pretty drawing of the dodecahedron.

Does this work because the dodecahedron graph is very symmetric?

Go to File>Open>planar1.grf. This is a planar graph where the nodes of one face (the red nodes) are fixed to a square, but the other nodes are in a random position. Click Move: The edges contract and eventually they will not cross any more.

Play with the other planar graphs in File>Open: try out all five platonic’solids, and the other, less regular planar graphs.

Hint: You can modify the graphs using the methods under the Edit menu: see Help.

Tutte proved the following nontrivial fact:

Nail the nodes of any face of a 3-connected planar graph to a convex polygon, and replace the edges by rubber bands. The equilibrium provides an embedding in the plane.

This embedding has very nice properties: all the edges are straight line segments, and all the faces are convex polygons.

Is this a good method to display planar 3-connected graphs?

Often it is, but there are some bad examples. Open File>Open>triangles.grf and click Move: This simple graph is drawn in the plane, but most of its nodes are crowded near the middle of the bottom. File>Open>pipe.grf gives a similar example.

What to do if a planar graph is not 3-connected?

We can add edges to it to make it 3-connected, and delete these at the end. As a special case, a tree can be drawn nicely by fixing its leaves. Try File>Open>tree1.grf...tree5.grf.

Is this a good method to display nonplanar graphs? Sometimes; check File>Open>petersen.grf to see a nice drawing of a very famous and very symmetric nonplanar graph, the "Petersen graph".
